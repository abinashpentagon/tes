Introduction: Call Option (CE)

A call option, often abbreviated as CE (Call European), is a type of option contract that gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy an underlying asset at a specified price (known as the strike price) within a specified time period. This type of option is used when the trader expects the price of the underlying asset to rise. There are two main activities in call options: buying and selling (writing).

Buying a Call Option

When you buy a call option, you pay a premium to the seller (writer) of the option. This premium is the price you pay for having the right to buy the underlying asset at the strike price.

Example of Buying a Call Option

Imagine you are interested in shares of Company XYZ, which are currently trading at ₹100 per share. You believe the price will rise over the next month.

  • Purchasing a Call Option:
    • You buy a call option with a strike price of ₹110, expiring in one month.
    • The premium for this option is ₹5 per share.
    • Each option typically represents 100 shares, so your total investment (premium) is ₹500 (₹5 × 100 shares).
  • Possible Outcomes:
    • Stock Price Rises Above ₹110: If Company XYZ’s stock price rises to ₹120 before the option expires, you can exercise your option to buy at ₹110. You can then sell the stock at the current market price of ₹120, making a profit. Your profit per share is ₹10 (₹120 - ₹110) minus the ₹5 premium paid, resulting in a net profit of ₹5 per share. Total profit: ₹500 (₹5 net profit per share × 100 shares).
    • Stock Price Remains Below ₹110: If Company XYZ’s stock price stays below ₹110, you will not exercise the option as it would result in a loss. Your loss is limited to the premium paid, which is ₹500.
Selling (Writing) a Call Option

When you sell or write a call option, you receive a premium from the buyer of the option. In return, you take on the obligation to sell the underlying asset at the strike price if the buyer chooses to exercise the option.

Example of Selling a Call Option

Suppose you own shares of Company XYZ, currently trading at ₹100 per share, and you believe the price will not rise significantly above ₹110 in the next month.

  • Selling a Call Option:
    • You sell a call option with a strike price of ₹110, expiring in one month.
    • You receive a premium of ₹5 per share.
    • Each option typically represents 100 shares, so you receive a total premium of ₹500 (₹5 × 100 shares).
  • Possible Outcomes:
    • Stock Price Stays Below ₹110: If Company XYZ’s stock price stays below ₹110, the option will expire worthless. You keep the entire premium of ₹500 as profit.
    • Stock Price Rises Above ₹110: If Company XYZ’s stock price rises above ₹110, the option buyer may exercise the option. You are obligated to sell the shares at ₹110, regardless of the current market price. If the stock price rises to ₹120, your effective loss per share is ₹5 (₹120 - ₹110), offset by the ₹5 premium received. Total profit/loss: You keep the premium of ₹500, but if you have to sell at ₹110 when the price is ₹120, you miss out on an additional ₹10 per share profit, resulting in a net position close to breakeven, considering the premium received.
Benefits and Risks of Selling Call Options
Benefits of Selling Call Options
  • Premium Income: You receive immediate income from the premium paid by the option buyer.
  • Profit from Stability: If the stock price remains below the strike price, you keep the premium as profit.
Risks of Selling Call Options
  • Unlimited Loss Potential: If the stock price rises significantly above the strike price, you could face substantial losses as you must sell the stock at a price lower than the market value.
  • Obligation to Sell: You are obligated to sell the underlying asset at the strike price if the option is exercised, which may result in missing out on higher market prices.
Conclusion

A call option (CE) provides traders with flexibility in their investment strategies. Buying call options allows you to profit from price increases with limited risk, while selling call options generates income but comes with higher risk. Understanding both buying and selling strategies, along with their associated risks and rewards, is crucial for making informed trading decisions.
