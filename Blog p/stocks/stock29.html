Introduction: Put Option (PE)

A put option, often abbreviated as PE (Put European), is a type of option contract that gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to sell an underlying asset at a specified price (known as the strike price) within a specified time period. This type of option is used when the trader expects the price of the underlying asset to fall. There are two main activities in put options: buying and selling (writing).

Buying a Put Option

When you buy a put option, you pay a premium to the seller (writer) of the option. This premium is the price you pay for having the right to sell the underlying asset at the strike price.

Example of Buying a Put Option

Imagine you are interested in shares of Company XYZ, which are currently trading at ₹100 per share. You believe the price will fall over the next month.

  • Purchasing a Put Option:
    • You buy a put option with a strike price of ₹90, expiring in one month.
    • The premium for this option is ₹5 per share.
    • Each option typically represents 100 shares, so your total investment (premium) is ₹500 (₹5 × 100 shares).
  • Possible Outcomes:
    • Stock Price Falls Below ₹90: If Company XYZ’s stock price falls to ₹80 before the option expires, you can exercise your option to sell at ₹90. You can then buy the stock at the current market price of ₹80, making a profit. Your profit per share is ₹10 (₹90 - ₹80) minus the ₹5 premium paid, resulting in a net profit of ₹5 per share. Total profit: ₹500 (₹5 net profit per share × 100 shares).
    • Stock Price Remains Above ₹90: If Company XYZ’s stock price stays above ₹90, you will not exercise the option as it would result in a loss. Your loss is limited to the premium paid, which is ₹500.
Selling (Writing) a Put Option

When you sell or write a put option, you receive a premium from the buyer of the option. In return, you take on the obligation to buy the underlying asset at the strike price if the buyer chooses to exercise the option.

Example of Selling a Put Option

Suppose you are interested in acquiring shares of Company XYZ, currently trading at ₹100 per share, but you believe the price might fall to around ₹90 in the next month.

  • Selling a Put Option:
    • You sell a put option with a strike price of ₹90, expiring in one month.
    • You receive a premium of ₹5 per share.
    • Each option typically represents 100 shares, so you receive a total premium of ₹500 (₹5 × 100 shares).
  • Possible Outcomes:
    • Stock Price Stays Above ₹90: If Company XYZ’s stock price stays above ₹90, the option will expire worthless. You keep the entire premium of ₹500 as profit.
    • Stock Price Falls Below ₹90: If Company XYZ’s stock price falls below ₹90, the option buyer may exercise the option. You are obligated to buy the shares at ₹90, regardless of the current market price. If the stock price falls to ₹80, your effective loss per share is ₹10 (₹90 - ₹80), offset by the ₹5 premium received. Total profit/loss: You keep the premium of ₹500, but if you have to buy at ₹90 when the price is ₹80, you incur a net loss of ₹5 per share, resulting in a total loss of ₹500 (₹5 net loss per share × 100 shares).
Benefits and Risks of Selling Put Options
Benefits of Selling Put Options
  • Premium Income: You receive immediate income from the premium paid by the option buyer.
  • Potential to Acquire Stock at a Discount: If you are willing to buy the stock at the strike price, selling put options can be a way to potentially acquire the stock at a discount (strike price minus the premium received).
Risks of Selling Put Options
  • Significant Loss Potential: If the stock price falls significantly below the strike price, you could face substantial losses as you must buy the stock at a price higher than its market value.
  • Obligation to Buy: You are obligated to buy the underlying asset at the strike price if the option is exercised, which may result in purchasing the stock at an unfavorable price.
Conclusion

A put option (PE) provides traders with flexibility in their investment strategies. Buying put options allows you to profit from price decreases with limited risk, while selling put options generates income but comes with higher risk. Understanding both buying and selling strategies, along with their associated risks and rewards, is crucial for making informed trading decisions.
